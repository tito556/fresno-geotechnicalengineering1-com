Seismic in Fresno

Sound ground. Sound decisions.

LEARN MORE

Seismic engineering in Fresno encompasses a specialized suite of geotechnical and structural services designed to evaluate, mitigate, and design against earthquake-induced hazards. Located in California's Central Valley, Fresno sits within a seismically active region influenced by the San Andreas Fault system to the west and numerous lesser-known faults crisscrossing the valley floor. The importance of these services cannot be overstated: a thorough understanding of local seismic hazards directly informs building safety, infrastructure resilience, and long-term urban planning. From evaluating the potential for ground failure to designing structures that can ride out strong shaking, this category covers the full spectrum of earthquake risk management. For property developers and public agencies alike, integrating a soil liquefaction analysis early in the design phase is often the first critical step toward a resilient project.

The geology beneath Fresno presents a unique set of challenges that demand careful seismic consideration. Much of the city is underlain by Quaternary alluvial deposits—loose sands, silts, and clays deposited by the San Joaquin River and its tributaries. These young, unconsolidated sediments are particularly susceptible to amplification of seismic waves, which can intensify shaking at the surface. More critically, the presence of shallow groundwater in many areas creates ideal conditions for soil liquefaction, where saturated granular soils lose strength and behave like a liquid during an earthquake. This phenomenon can cause foundation failures, excessive settlement, and lateral spreading. A detailed soil liquefaction analysis is therefore not just a regulatory checkbox but a fundamental necessity for projects in Fresno's floodplain and low-lying zones.

Regulatory compliance in Fresno is governed by a hierarchy of codes rooted in California's rigorous seismic safety framework. The California Building Code (CBC), which incorporates the International Building Code (IBC) with state-specific amendments, mandates seismic design for virtually all new construction and substantial alterations. These codes reference ASCE 7 standards for minimum design loads and require site-specific geotechnical investigations that characterize seismic hazards. For essential facilities like hospitals and emergency response centers, the California Office of Statewide Health Planning and Development (OSHPD) enforces even stricter criteria. Additionally, the City of Fresno's local ordinances may require seismic evaluations for certain retrofit projects, particularly for unreinforced masonry buildings. On complex or high-risk sites, advanced techniques such as base isolation seismic design can be employed to exceed code minimums and achieve performance objectives beyond life safety.

The types of projects that require seismic engineering services in Fresno are remarkably diverse. High-rise commercial buildings and multi-family residential structures must account for seismic forces in their structural design, often benefiting from base isolation seismic design to protect both the building and its contents. Infrastructure projects—bridges, overpasses, and water treatment plants—depend on seismic resilience to maintain lifeline functions after an earthquake. For large-scale developments or urban planning initiatives, seismic microzonation studies provide a block-by-block understanding of hazard variability, guiding land-use decisions and foundation design. Even smaller projects like single-family homes in liquefaction-prone zones require a basic seismic hazard evaluation to ensure safe and insurable construction. Each project type demands a tailored approach, blending regulatory compliance with performance-based engineering.

Frequently asked questions

What is the difference between seismic hazard and seismic risk?

Seismic hazard refers to the natural probability of earthquake-induced ground shaking, fault rupture, or liquefaction at a specific location, based purely on geology and seismicity. Seismic risk, on the other hand, combines that hazard with the vulnerability and exposure of the built environment—considering what could be damaged, the consequences, and the economic or human losses. Hazard is the earthquake's potential; risk is what we stand to lose.

How do local soil conditions in Fresno affect earthquake shaking?

Fresno's deep alluvial soils, deposited by rivers over millennia, tend to amplify seismic waves, making ground shaking stronger and longer-lasting than on bedrock sites. Soft clays and loose sands can trap and magnify certain frequencies, while shallow groundwater increases liquefaction susceptibility. A site-specific geotechnical investigation quantifies these effects through shear wave velocity testing and dynamic analysis, directly influencing structural design parameters.

When is a seismic microzonation study required instead of a standard site investigation?

A seismic microzonation study is typically required for large-scale developments, urban planning projects, or linear infrastructure like pipelines and roadways where ground conditions vary significantly across the site. It provides a detailed map of shaking potential, liquefaction susceptibility, and landslide risk at a neighborhood or district scale, allowing for optimized land use, targeted mitigation, and more precise building code application than a single-point investigation.

What are the first steps in evaluating seismic safety for an existing building in Fresno?

The process begins with a screening-level review, often using ASCE 41 or FEMA P-154 checklists, to identify potential structural weaknesses. This is followed by a detailed seismic evaluation, which includes reviewing original construction documents, conducting field investigations of foundations and lateral systems, and performing analytical modeling to assess expected performance. The findings guide retrofit recommendations or further detailed analysis to meet current code standards.
