Shallow Foundation Design in Fresno — Geotechnical Approach for the Central Valley

Sound ground. Sound decisions.

LEARN MORE

Fresno sits at roughly 308 feet above sea level, but what matters for a foundation is the first 30 feet below grade. We have worked the alluvial fans of the Kings and San Joaquin rivers long enough to know that bearing soil here can shift from dense sand to fat clay in less than a block. A shallow foundation design in Fresno has to resolve that lateral variability before we even talk about column loads. Our approach ties site-specific SPT data and lab classification to a bearing capacity model that meets ASCE 7-22 and Chapter 18 of the IBC. No two lots are identical. We treat each subgrade as its own problem, because in this valley, that is exactly what it is.

Allowable bearing pressure in Fresno can vary 50% across a single building pad. The subgrade tells you the number — your job is to listen.

Methodology and scope

Last year we reviewed a three-story medical office near Herndon Avenue, where the owner had a geotech report from 2005. The borings showed stiff clay to 8 feet, but the adjacent lot had been compacted fill with a different moisture history. We ran two additional test pits and a set of Atterberg limits. The plasticity index jumped from 18 to 34 across 40 feet. That changed the net allowable bearing pressure by almost 900 psf. A shallow foundation design in Fresno demands that level of resolution. We check for collapsible soils, because loose alluvium in the eastern edges of the county can lose structure when wetted. We check for expansive potential, because the Clovis-Fresno transition zone has mapped units with high shrink-swell. And we verify the groundwater depth, which in wet years can rise to within 6 feet of the finished floor in parts of the 93720 and 93725 ZIP codes. For projects with marginal bearing we often coordinate with a ground improvement program using stone columns before finalizing the footing geometry.

Local considerations

A project near the Tower District sits on older, overconsolidated clay. Move two miles south toward the industrial corridor east of Highway 99, and you are on recent floodplain deposits with higher compressibility and lower undrained shear strength. The risk in the first case is mostly shrink-swell. The risk in the second is differential settlement that can crack a slab-on-grade within the first three wet-dry cycles. We have seen footing excavations left open overnight collapse because the contractor did not account for the rapid moisture loss in Fresno's 100-degree summer afternoons. A shallow foundation design in Fresno that skips a plasticity analysis and a settlement estimate for the actual column spacing is gambling. We calculate immediate settlement using Schmertmann's method and check consolidation settlement where clay thickness exceeds 5 feet. If the ratio of bearing pressure to preconsolidation stress is above 0.9, we recommend either a deepened footing or a mat.

Frequently asked questions

What soil types in Fresno are most problematic for shallow foundations?

The two main concerns are expansive clays, especially in the transition zone between Clovis and Fresno, and loose alluvial silts east of Highway 41. The clays can swell enough to lift a lightly loaded slab. The silts can collapse when wetted. Our standard protocol runs Atterberg limits and a collapse potential test on any sample with dry density below 90 pcf.

How much does a shallow foundation design cost for a commercial building in Fresno?

A complete shallow foundation design package — field investigation, lab testing, bearing capacity analysis, and stamped report — typically runs between US$1,720 and US$3,580. The range depends on number of borings, lab tests requested, and whether we need to model settlement for irregular column grids.

Do you need to test for liquefaction for a shallow foundation in Fresno County?

Most of Fresno's urban core sits outside the high-hazard liquefaction zones mapped by the California Geological Survey. However, if the project is near the San Joaquin River corridor or in areas with shallow groundwater and loose sands, we run an SPT-based liquefaction screening per the NCEER method. It is a quick check that can save months of remediation later.
