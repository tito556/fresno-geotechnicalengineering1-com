Triaxial Testing in Fresno: Shear Strength Parameters for Foundation Design

Sound ground. Sound decisions.

LEARN MORE

The soils of northwest Fresno near the San Joaquin River tell a completely different story than the hardpan encountered east of Highway 41 toward Clovis. River deposits bring silts and loose sands with high water tables, while the eastern terraces hold stiff, overconsolidated clays that can stand on near-vertical cuts. For a geotechnical engineer designing a warehouse foundation or a stormwater basin, guessing the shear strength from blow counts alone introduces risk that no contractor wants to absorb. The triaxial test resolves that uncertainty by measuring cohesion and friction angle under controlled drainage conditions, giving the project team a failure envelope they can defend to the building official. When we run a triaxial program in our Fresno lab, the sample is saturated, consolidated to field stress, and sheared at a rate slow enough to let pore pressures equalize—this is standard practice for the silty clays found across the 99 corridor. For projects near Highway 180 where loose alluvium raises liquefaction concerns, we pair the triaxial with CPT soundings to calibrate cyclic resistance ratios directly against in-situ tip resistance.

A triaxial test doesn't just give you a friction angle; it gives you the stress path that predicts whether your excavation bottom will heave before the mat pour.

Methodology and scope

Fresno sits on the eastern edge of the Central Valley aquifer system, where groundwater extraction has caused historic subsidence of over 10 feet in some areas since the 1920s. That long-term settlement changes the in-situ stress state of the soil column, and a triaxial program designed without accounting for preconsolidation from past pumping will underestimate stiffness. Our lab runs consolidated-undrained (CU) tests with pore pressure measurement on Shelby tube samples retrieved from depths between 15 and 60 feet, applying backpressure saturation until Skempton's B parameter exceeds 0.95. For free-draining sands encountered in the Friant formation near Millerton Lake, we switch to consolidated-drained (CD) protocols under ASTM D7181. Every specimen is extruded, trimmed, and set up on a load frame with electronic pressure-volume controllers that log deviator stress, axial strain, and volume change at 0.1-second intervals. The report includes Mohr-Coulomb parameters, stress paths in p-q space, and secant modulus at 50% strain—deliverables that structural engineers can push directly into PLAXIS or FLAC models without re-interpretation.

Local considerations

Samples that dry out during transport from the drill rig to the lab lose matric suction, and the triaxial result will look like a normally consolidated clay even when the deposit is heavily overconsolidated. In Fresno summers, where daytime highs average 98°F in July, a Shelby tube left in the sun for two hours can ruin a $3,000 drilling day. We insist on field-sealed tubes with waxed ends and overnight delivery to our climate-controlled receiving area. Another pattern we see repeatedly: contractors order unconsolidated-undrained (UU) tests for a slope stability problem because the unit price is lower, but UU doesn't give effective stress parameters, so the engineer can't model pore pressure dissipation during staged excavation. For any cut deeper than 10 feet in the Friant Kern Canal zone, CU with pore pressure measurement is the minimum defensible standard. The City of Fresno Public Works plan check increasingly asks for effective stress strength envelopes on commercial projects over 5,000 square feet—showing up without them delays the permit.

Frequently asked questions

What type of triaxial test do I need for a warehouse foundation in Fresno?

For shallow foundations on the silty clays common along the Highway 99 corridor, a consolidated-undrained (CU) test with pore pressure measurement under ASTM D4767 is the standard. It provides effective stress cohesion and friction angle that your geotechnical engineer needs for bearing capacity calculations. If the site has free-draining sands above the water table, a consolidated-drained (CD) test per ASTM D7181 may be more appropriate.

How much does a triaxial test program cost in Fresno?

A three-specimen CU triaxial program with full reporting typically ranges from US$1,710 to US$2,800 depending on sample depth, required confining pressures, and whether multi-stage testing is feasible on a single specimen. We provide a fixed-price proposal after reviewing the boring logs and project geotechnical report requirements.

How long does it take to get triaxial test results?

Standard turnaround is 10 to 14 business days from sample receipt. Consolidated-drained tests on low-permeability clays can take longer because the shear stage must be slow enough to prevent pore pressure buildup. We can expedite to 7 days for an additional fee when the concrete pour is on hold.

What sample quality do you need for a reliable triaxial test?

The test is only as good as the sample. We need undisturbed Shelby tube samples (3-inch diameter minimum for cohesive soils) that have been sealed with wax immediately after extrusion in the field and kept at field moisture content. Disturbed samples, bag samples, or tubes that show evidence of drying or freezing will not produce reliable strength parameters.
